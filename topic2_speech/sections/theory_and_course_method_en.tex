\section{Theory and Two Template Methods}

\subsection{Our method: short-time FFT templates}

Our method corresponds to \texttt{strict\_course\_fft} in the code. Its modeling idea is direct: the useful evidence for the initial voiced consonant lies in a short segment near the word onset, and different classes have different local magnitude-spectrum prototypes. Recording level, speaker identity, and the following vowel all introduce variation, so the method compares normalized local spectral shape rather than whole-word energy.

Let $x_i[n]$ be the waveform of sample $i$. The frame length is $L=20$ ms, the hop size is $H=10$ ms, and the FFT size is $N=512$. The spectrum of frame $m$ is
\[
X_{i,m}[k] = \sum_{n=0}^{L-1} x_i[n+mH]w[n]e^{-j2\pi kn/N},
\qquad k=0,\dots,N-1,
\]
where $w[n]$ is a Hamming window. Only the one-sided magnitude spectrum is kept, and each frame is normalized by its maximum magnitude. If a candidate block starts at frame $r$ and spans $M$ frames, its descriptor is
\[
P_i^{(r)}[k]
=\frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=0}^{M-1}
\frac{|X_{i,r+m}[k]|}{\max_{\kappa}|X_{i,r+m}[\kappa]|},
\qquad k=0,\dots,N/2.
\]
The descriptor $P_i^{(r)}$ is then normalized by its $L_2$ norm. This step suppresses amplitude variation and keeps the shape of the short-time spectrum.

Templates are estimated from the training data. For each class $c$, the method first selects the maximum-energy $M$-frame block from each positive sample and denotes it by $P_i^{(\star)}$. It then averages these descriptors and normalizes the result:
\[
T_c=\frac{\sum_{i:y_i=c}P_i^{(\star)}}
{\left\|\sum_{i:y_i=c}P_i^{(\star)}\right\|_2}.
\]
The development set selects the block length from $M\in\{3,4,5,6\}$. On the current dataset, the selected value is $M=5$. The final templates are then re-estimated from same-class samples in the training and development sets. The whole procedure uses only framing, FFT, averaging, and normalized correlation, which keeps it close to the spectral analysis tools used in class.

At test time, the method applies the same descriptor to every candidate block and computes
\[
\rho_{i,c}^{(r)}
=\frac{\langle P_i^{(r)},T_c\rangle}
{\|P_i^{(r)}\|_2\|T_c\|_2},
\qquad
s_{i,c}=\max_r\rho_{i,c}^{(r)}.
\]
The predicted class is $\arg\max_c s_{i,c}$. The reproducible algorithm is given in Appendix~\ref{alg:strict-course}.

\subsection{AI reimplementation: filterbank-based local templates and autocorrelation}

The AI reimplementation corresponds to \texttt{course\_filterbank\_corr}. It remains a template method, but it adds three changes: mel filterbanks for spectral compression, energy-based localization of a local onset window, and both positive and negative templates for each class. Its basic idea is that the most useful evidence lies near the word onset, a class should resemble its own positive samples and differ from the other three classes, and the energy-envelope autocorrelation can help separate sustained frication from short stop releases.

The method first uses 25 ms frames and 10 ms hops to compute the power spectrum and then applies 40 mel triangular filters to obtain a log-mel vector
\[
L_i[m,q]=\log\left(\sum_k |X_{i,m}[k]|^2 B_q[k]+\epsilon\right),
\qquad q=1,\dots,40.
\]
Frame energy is defined as
\[
e_i[m]=\log\left(\frac{1}{40}\sum_q \exp L_i[m,q]+\epsilon\right).
\]
The code smooths $e_i[m]$ with a 5-frame moving average and uses the median of the first several frames as the baseline. The onset estimate $o_i$ is the first position whose value exceeds the baseline by 0.22. The candidate window has length 16 frames, starts 2 frames before the onset estimate, and is also evaluated under three offsets $\Delta\in\{-2,0,2\}$:
\[
W_i^{(\Delta)}=
L_i[o_i-2+\Delta: o_i-2+\Delta+15,:].
\]

Each candidate window is divided into $S=3$ segments. The method keeps both the raw segment means and the segment means after subtracting the baseline computed from the first 2 frames, and concatenates them into a local spectral vector
\[
\phi_i^{(\Delta)}=
\left[
\mu_1(W),\mu_2(W),\mu_3(W),
\mu_1(W-\bar W_{\mathrm{pre}}),\mu_2(W-\bar W_{\mathrm{pre}}),\mu_3(W-\bar W_{\mathrm{pre}})
\right].
\]
Each $\mu_s$ is 40-dimensional, so $\phi_i^{(\Delta)}\in\mathbb{R}^{240}$. The method also computes a 24-lag normalized autocorrelation vector $a_i^{(\Delta)}$ from the energy envelope of the same window.

For each class $c$, the training set produces four templates: positive and negative local spectral templates $P_c^+$ and $P_c^-$, and positive and negative autocorrelation templates $A_c^+$ and $A_c^-$. These templates are the class-wise means of positive and negative training features. At test time, the score for each offset is
\[
\begin{aligned}
g_{i,c}^{(\Delta)}=&\;0.65\left[
\cos(\phi_i^{(\Delta)},P_c^+)-\cos(\phi_i^{(\Delta)},P_c^-)
\right]\\
&+0.35\left[
\cos(a_i^{(\Delta)},A_c^+)-\cos(a_i^{(\Delta)},A_c^-)
\right],\\
s_{i,c}=&\;\max_{\Delta\in\{-2,0,2\}}g_{i,c}^{(\Delta)}.
\end{aligned}
\]

Compared with our method, this score adds negative-template contrast, autocorrelation, and a more explicit onset-localization step. In the scope of this report, it serves as the main AI reimplementation of the spectral description and detection stages. It still keeps an interpretable template structure, but the representation and the scoring rule are more complex. The reproducible algorithm is given in Appendix~\ref{alg:course-filterbank}.

\subsection{MATLAB demonstration}

To stay consistent with the spectral analysis tools used in class, we also implement a matching MATLAB demonstration. The core score remains
\[
\texttt{score}=\frac{\texttt{mag}^\top \texttt{template}}{\|\texttt{mag}\|_2\|\texttt{template}\|_2}.
\]
The demonstration visualizes the short-time spectral templates and the correlation score matrix for four representative samples, which makes the relation between the templates and the speech signal easy to inspect. The formal evaluation, however, uses the shared data split, block-length selection on the development set, and template re-estimation from the training and development sets.
