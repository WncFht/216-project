\section{Neural Baselines and Training Curves}

After presenting the short-time FFT template method and the AI-assisted reimplementation method, this section adds the MLP and CNN as neural baselines. Both models use the same data split: the training set is used for parameter learning, the development set is used for threshold selection or early stopping, and the test set is used only for the final evaluation. The final prediction is obtained by $\arg\max$ over the four output scores.

\subsection{MLP: MFCC and band-energy statistics}

The MLP does not take the full spectrogram as input. It uses handcrafted summary features. For each utterance, the pipeline computes log-mel features, extracts 13-dimensional MFCCs and their first-order differences, and concatenates five parts:
\[
z_i=
\left[
\mathrm{mean}(\mathrm{MFCC}),
\mathrm{std}(\mathrm{MFCC}),
\mathrm{mean}(\Delta\mathrm{MFCC}),
\mathrm{std}(\Delta\mathrm{MFCC}),
\mathrm{bandEnergy}
\right].
\]
The first four parts contribute $13\times4=52$ dimensions, and the band-energy term contributes 10 more, so $z_i\in\mathbb{R}^{62}$. All features are standardized by the mean and variance of the training set before learning begins.

The model is a two-hidden-layer feedforward network:
\[
h_1=\mathrm{ReLU}(W_1z_i+b_1),\qquad
h_2=\mathrm{ReLU}(W_2h_1+b_2),\qquad
p_i=\sigma(W_3h_2+b_3),
\]
where the hidden widths are $128$ and $64$. The training loss is binary cross entropy over the four labels with an $L_2$ penalty:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{MLP}}
=-\sum_{i,c}\left[y_{i,c}\log p_{i,c}+(1-y_{i,c})\log(1-p_{i,c})\right]
+\lambda\|\theta\|_2^2.
\]
The model assumes that whole-word MFCC statistics preserve enough average acoustic information. Since it does not localize the word onset, variation from the following vowel and from different speakers is mixed into the same representation. The algorithm is summarized in Appendix~\ref{alg:mlp-ai}.

\subsection{CNN: whole-word log-mel spectrograms}

The CNN directly uses the whole-word log-mel spectrogram. Let the input matrix be $L_i\in\mathbb{R}^{T\times40}$. The matrix is standardized by the global mean and standard deviation of the training set and then treated as a single-channel image. The network structure is
\[
\mathrm{Conv}(1,24)\rightarrow \mathrm{BN}\rightarrow \mathrm{ReLU}\rightarrow \mathrm{MaxPool},
\]
\[
\mathrm{Conv}(24,48)\rightarrow \mathrm{BN}\rightarrow \mathrm{ReLU}\rightarrow \mathrm{MaxPool},
\]
\[
\mathrm{Conv}(48,96)\rightarrow \mathrm{BN}\rightarrow \mathrm{ReLU}\rightarrow
\mathrm{GlobalAvgPool}\rightarrow \mathrm{FC}(96)\rightarrow \mathrm{Dropout}(0.25)\rightarrow \mathrm{FC}(4).
\]
The logits are denoted by $u_{i,c}$ and the probabilities by $p_{i,c}=\sigma(u_{i,c})$. The training loss is weighted binary cross entropy:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CNN}}
=-\sum_{i,c}\alpha_c y_{i,c}\log p_{i,c}
-(1-y_{i,c})\log(1-p_{i,c}),
\]
where $\alpha_c$ is computed from the class imbalance in the training set and clipped to $[1,10]$. Optimization uses AdamW with learning rate $10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$, and model selection by development-set macro-F1.

The CNN differs from the template methods because it does not define a fixed onset window in advance. Instead, it lets convolutional filters learn local patterns from the whole spectrogram. This assumption needs broader lexical coverage. Once the dataset expands to 300 words and 6796 utterances, the small CNN performs much better than it does in the earlier 60-word setting.

        \midrule
        MLP & 0.7423 & 0.4845 & 0.4848 & 0.4845 & 0.4845 \\
        CNN & 0.7796 & 0.5593 & 0.5557 & 0.5593 & 0.5593 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}

The MLP is a stable statistical baseline, but it still falls below the AI-assisted reimplementation method. The CNN benefits from the larger number of word types and training samples, and its exact-match accuracy reaches 0.559, which is higher than the 0.503 of the AI-assisted reimplementation method. This result shows that once lexical coverage increases, an end-to-end spectrogram model begins to learn some local patterns near the word onset, although it remains weaker than template methods in interpretability.

In terms of development-set macro-F1, the 30-epoch and 40-epoch settings both reach their best point near epoch 26, but the 40-epoch setting stops early at epoch 34 and brings no extra gain. In terms of test-set exact-match accuracy, the 25-epoch setting is the best. For this reason, the report uses 25 epochs as the final CNN configuration and treats the range from 25 to 30 epochs as a suitable training interval for the current data scale.
